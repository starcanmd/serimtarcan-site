The longer version

I started in clinical medicine and kept following two questions. Why does good care so often fail to reach the people who need it, and what would it take to fix that at scale.

The hardest lesson came at home for me. I lost my father to the same gaps I had been studying. After that, the clinic would never be enough for me. The system itself needed care, and I wanted to help build it.

This moved me from seeing patients as a physician to co-founding a digital health company for refugees, building AI products inside a large health system and a startup, advising health systems around the globe, and supporting founders as an investor.

Now I am heading to Stanford GSB to sharpen the operator and the founder in me. Along the way, I will write The Rounds on Substack, where I take apart how US healthcare really works for people coming from outside the system.

The plan from here is to build. A product where medicine and AI meet, amplified by someone who has sat on every side of the table. Still shaping what it could be. If that is the kind of thing you are working on too, I'm an email away.

On This Design

The background shifts in color with the time of day, and your cursor leaves soft brush strokes as it moves, loose and visible, the way the Impressionists painted. The phase of the day you land on depends on the hour you arrive. And each phase features a unique piece by an Impressionist composer.

The compositions on this site are recorded, produced, and published by Gregor Quendel, and included by courtesy of classicals.de.

Why This Design

The Impressionists have always impressed me, both the painters and the composers. Less for what they made, more for how they went about their craft.

Painters used loose strokes instead of clean lines and left the picture unfinished. The composers did the same in sound. A piece could just trail off instead of tying up. They cared more about how a place felt than how exact it looked. Most of our days are the same too. Small, daily mistakes only matter up close. If we step back and look at the entire canvas, those imperfections lose their weight.

They painted ordinary things, a café, a garden, a stretch of river. And they'd paint the same scene over and over to catch the light as it moved. The scene was never the same for them. Neither is anything else worth paying attention to in life. Every passing moment is there for us to observe its unique beauty until it changes for good.

And lastly, they challenged what counted as good art by institutions and society and created on their own terms. It reminds me to carve my own path, trust my own experiences, and stay true to myself as much as I can. We don't have to follow the script to make something real.

I think these are the real reasons why they stuck with me. It's how I've tried to live. I skipped the path I was supposed to take, and I'm learning to be okay with a story that keeps being reshaped. This site works the same way. It includes a few things worth noticing, but not a complete, finished account of me.
